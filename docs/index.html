Introduction

For our project we will consider the following logic puzzle.
Assume we have 2 agents, Alice and Bob, both are perfect logicians. Let us pick 2 random positive integers smaller than 100. We tell Alice the sum of these numbers and we tell Bob the product of these numbers. Both know this. Then the following conversations occurs between the agents:

Bob: I do not know the numbers.
Alice: I do not know the numbers.
Bob: I do not know the numbers.
Alice: I do not know the numbers.
Bob: I do not know the numbers.
Alice: I do not know the numbers.
Bob: I do not know the numbers.
Alice: I do not know the numbers.
Bob: I do not know the numbers.
Alice: I do not know the numbers.
Bob: I do not know the numbers.
Alice: I do not know the numbers.
Bob: I do not know the numbers.
Alice: I do not know the numbers.

Bob: I do know the numbers!

What are the numbers?

The puzzle is very similar to Rinekes birthday problem discussed during the course. However, there is a key differenct. In Rinekes birthday puzzle we assume the identity of one of the agents. However, in this puzzle we are a third agent who has no impact on the conversation and is only able to listen. Later on we will also consider this puzzle from the perspective of the agents. The problem looks quite intimidating at first, but the problem reduces relatively quickly when using a computer. We will start by noting that the pairs of numbers have no inherent ordering to them, hence the pairs (3,7) and (7,3) are equivalent. From here on we will denote each pair as (x,y) with x<=y.

For the sake of explanation, let us assume that the random numbers are chosen between [1,6) instead of [1,100). We will use this reduced example to show how we can reduce the set of possible number combinations for each anounchement.

This can be repeated to obtain all nodes removed during each step. However, we would like to note that the problem is only solvable for for the 3rd agent in case only a single pair is removed by an announcement. If there are multiple pairs that are removed by the anounchement then the 3rd agent only knows that it is one of these pairs of numbers but not which one, as is the case in our example.

As one might imagine this indicates that there are quite significant constraints on the original problem for a solution to exists for a given (fixed) number of 'Agent x does not know the numbers'. Hence we will also be looking an extention of the problem in which we consider ourselves to be one of the agents with knowledge about the received product or sum.

From the perspective of an playing agent.

Let's play the game out of Bob's perspective and assume that the number games is played with x, y being in a range of [1,6)] and we receive a number NB which is 4. Alice receives a number NA but this number is unknown for us. We know that x * y = NB and that 0 < x, y < 6. There are two possible combinations of (x, y) possible which are (2, 2) and (1, 4).

If the numbers are (2, 2), then NA = 4 and Alice would consider (2, 2) or (3, 1) as possible. Similarly, if the numbers are (4, 1) then NA = 5 and Alice would consider (4, 1) and (3, 2) as possible. Either way, we know that Alice does not know the combination.

We considers it possible that (2, 2) with NA = 4 and Alice considering (2, 2) and (3, 1). However, if (3, 1) then NB = 3 which means that (x, y) must be (3, 1) (The only possible numbers with a product of 3). Therefore Alice would consider it possible that we know (3, 1). We consider it possible that Alice considers it possible that we know the combination.

Now, we announce that we do not know (x, y). Do we still know that Alice does not know (x, y)? We consider it possible that (2, 2) with NA = 4. If NA = 4 then Alice would know that either (2, 2) or (3, 1). However, Alice knows that (3, 1) is not possible because we stated that we do not know (If (3, 1) then NB = 3 and we would know since the only possible product is (1, 3)). Therefore Alice would eliminate (3, 1) and conclude the only possible option which is (2, 2). Therefore should we should consider it possible that Alice knows (2, 2).

Do we know that Alice knows (x, y)? We consider (2, 2) and (4, 1) as possible. As already explained, if (2, 2), then Alice would know (2, 2), after we states that we do not know. If on the other hand (4, 1), then NA = 5 and Alice would consider (4, 1) or (3, 2) as possible. However, if (3, 2) then NB = 6 and the only possible products are (3, 2). Alice can eliminate (3, 2) because we stated that we do not know. Therefore Alice would know (4, 1). So we know that Alice either knows (2, 2) or of (1, 4).
